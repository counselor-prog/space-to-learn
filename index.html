Study smarter

A few strategies do most of the work. We'll help you find yours.

Decades of learning science point to the same small set of strategies. Here's what actually moves the needle, in order of impact.

01

Retrieval practice

Testing yourself, not re-reading, is what builds lasting memory. Close the book and try to recall before you check. Try it now: write down everything you just read from memory. The gaps you find are your actual study list.

02

Spaced review

A little, often, beats a lot, rarely. Spreading review out over days and weeks is what makes it stick long-term. In practice: review new material on day one, then day three, then day seven. The growing gaps between sessions are what build durable memory.

03

Interleaving

Mixing related topics in one sitting, rather than blocking by subject, trains you to recognize which concept applies when. Try studying a cardiology and a renal case back-to-back instead of five cardiology cases in a row.

04

Dual coding

Pairing words with diagrams, timelines, or sketches helps information take a second, more durable route into memory. After reading about a mechanism, draw it from memory before checking. The act of drawing consolidates what the reading only introduced.

05

Elaboration

Explaining the "why" behind a fact, and connecting it to what you already know, makes it far easier to recall later. Ask yourself: why does this mechanism work this way? What would break if it didn't? Connecting the why turns a fact into something you can actually use.

Protect the hours you have

Time management that survives a real week

Most time management advice assumes a predictable schedule. Yours isn't one. Here's what actually holds up, plus a real tool to build your own week.

01

Time block, don't to-do list

A list tells you what to do. A block tells you when. Without a when, the hardest task quietly gets pushed to tomorrow, every day.

02

Protect one recovery block daily

Treat rest as a scheduled block, not whatever time is left over. Leftover time is usually zero.

03

Batch by energy, not just topic

Put your hardest thinking in your highest energy hours. Save admin, review, and flashcards for the dips.

04

Plan the week in minutes, not hours

An elaborate plan you abandon by Wednesday is worse than a rough one you actually follow.

What works

Study strategies for Indigenous students

I've worked directly with Indigenous students in northern communities, in classrooms, in one-on-one sessions, and alongside Elders at cultural events. That experience shaped how I think about support: not as something you deliver from a distance, but something you build together, on the community's terms.

These aren't generic tips with an Indigenous label slapped on. They're approaches grounded in how many Indigenous students actually learn best, backed by what researchers and Elders have observed for decades. If some of these feel natural to you, that's not a coincidence.

A framework worth knowing: Two-Eyed Seeing (Etuaptmumk). Developed by Mi'kmaq Elder Albert Marshall, this idea is simple: use one eye to see through Indigenous ways of knowing, and the other to see through Western ones. Use both together. In practice, it means you don't have to leave your cultural knowledge at the door to succeed academically. You bring both. A nursing student can hold clinical anatomy and traditional knowledge about the body at the same time. A social work student can cite an academic theory and a teaching from an Elder in the same paper. You are not choosing between two worlds. You are stronger because you have access to both.
01

Speak it, don't just reread it

Instead of rereading your notes, explain the concept out loud: to yourself, a friend, or even just recorded on your phone. Oral recall is not a workaround. For many Indigenous learners, it is the primary way knowledge has always been transmitted, and research confirms it beats passive rereading for retention.

02

Use storytelling as a memory tool

When you need to remember something complex, turn it into a story with a beginning, middle, and end. Give the concepts characters. Let them interact. This is not a childish technique. It is how oral cultures have preserved detailed, accurate knowledge across generations. Your brain is built for it.

03

Study in a circle, not a hierarchy

If you study in groups, try a setup where everyone contributes equally rather than one person teaching the others. Circle-based discussion, where each person shares their understanding, mirrors traditional learning spaces and tends to surface gaps and insights that a lecture format misses entirely.

04

Anchor abstract concepts to place or community

When a concept feels slippery, ask yourself: where does this show up in a community I know? In a landscape I've been in? Grounding theory in a real place or relationship is not just a memory trick. It is a legitimate epistemological move. It also makes your essays and presentations more specific and more compelling.

05

Treat Elders and Knowledge Keepers as academic resources

If your institution has an Elder-in-residence, that is not just a cultural gesture. It is an academic resource you are paying for and most students never use. Knowledge Keepers carry expertise that does not exist in textbooks. Conversations with them can legitimately inform your coursework, your research questions, and your understanding of the field.

06

Frame studying as collective, not competitive

Many Indigenous value systems treat knowledge as something held together, not accumulated individually. If the competitive framing of university feels wrong to you, it may be because it is, at least for how you learn best. Find study partners who approach it as shared responsibility. You do not have to perform isolation to prove you are serious.

If you're in medicine

What nobody tells you about surviving med school

The first month, you'll feel like you've got it. The notes are organized, you're keeping up, and it feels like undergrad. Just more of it. That feeling is the trap. Somewhere around week six or eight, the volume tips. There's too much to reread. You stay up later to compensate. You reread the same page three times and retain none of it. You start wondering if you're the one who shouldn't be here. You're not. But what you're doing isn't working anymore.

The most counterintuitive finding in medical education: a study of USMLE Step 1 performance found that students who watched more educational videos scored lower, while students who did more practice questions scored higher. Watching feels like learning. Answering questions, especially ones you get wrong, actually is.
01

Stop reading about it. Start fighting with it.

The students who figure it out stop trying to absorb the material and start forcing themselves to recall it. Practice questions from week one, not week ten. Anki instead of notes. Teaching a concept to a study partner instead of highlighting it again. The shift feels uncomfortable at first because you're getting things wrong constantly. That's the point. Wrong answers in practice are how you stop getting them wrong on the exam.

02

You cannot learn everything. Make peace with that now.

The students who burn out earliest are usually the ones who refuse to triage. Everything feels high-yield when you're anxious. It isn't. First Aid, Pathoma, Sketchy. These exist because the field has already figured out what matters most for boards. Spending four hours mastering one obscure concept while twenty common ones go unreviewed is not thoroughness. It's a coping mechanism dressed up as studying.

03

Boards is a marathon you've already started.

Step 1 and the MCCQE aren't exams you study for six weeks before you write. They're exams you've been building toward since day one. Students who open Anki in first year and keep it running through second year arrive at dedicated study with two years of spaced repetition already in their memory. Students who wait until dedicated period are trying to compress that into six weeks. Start the clock now, even if it's just thirty cards a day.

04

Clerkship will feel like starting over. That's normal.

The jump from studying patients to standing in front of one is jarring in a way no amount of reading prepares you for. You'll feel underprepared. You'll second-guess everything you say. So does every other clerk. The students who come out of clerkship stronger aren't the ones who stopped feeling like imposters. They're the ones who learned to act anyway, ask questions without apology, and treat every patient encounter as a retrieval exercise rather than a performance.

05

The culture will tell you not to struggle out loud. Don't listen.

Depression and burnout rates in medical students run at roughly double the general population. The culture still makes it hard to say you're not okay. There's a particular cruelty in training people to help patients ask for help while quietly making it shameful to ask for it yourself. If you'd tell a patient to see someone, the logic doesn't stop applying when it's you. Getting support isn't a detour from becoming a good doctor. For a lot of students, it's part of how they get there.

06

Your study group is your survival system.

The students who isolate and grind alone are not working harder. They're missing the single most powerful retrieval tool available, which is explaining something to another person until they understand it. A good study group isn't a social consolation prize. It's where gaps get caught, concepts get stress-tested, and the weight of the program gets shared. Find people who approach it as a collective problem, not a competition. They exist in every cohort.
