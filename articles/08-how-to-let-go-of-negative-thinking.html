How to let go of negative thinking

At some point, most students hit a wall that has nothing to do with how hard they're studying. The problem isn't the material — it's the thinking that's running on a loop in the background. The voice that says you're behind, you're not smart enough, you should have started earlier, everyone else has it figured out.

That kind of thinking isn't just unpleasant. It actively gets in the way of learning. A mind that's caught in a spiral of self-criticism is not a mind that's absorbing new information.

What I've found, working with students over many years, is that the most useful thing isn't to fight the thinking or try to replace it with positive thoughts. It's to learn how to let it go. And that's a skill, not a personality trait. It can be practised.

Here is a five-step process you can use any time you feel overwhelmed by your thinking. The acronym is PAUSE — because that's exactly what you're doing: pausing before the thinking pulls you further in.

P
Pause — and breathe

The moment you notice you're overwhelmed, stop what you're doing and take a few slow, deep breaths. Not to fix anything — just to give your nervous system a signal that it's safe to slow down. Breathing is the fastest tool you have for coming back to the present moment. While you're breathing, simply notice that you're thinking. Don't try to change the thoughts yet. Just observe: there is thinking happening right now.

A
Ask yourself an honest question

Once you've taken a breath, ask yourself this: Is this thinking making me feel the way I want to feel? Is it helping me, or is it just adding to the suffering? You don't have to analyse the thoughts or figure out where they came from. You just need to be honest with yourself about what they're doing to you. If the answer is that they're not helping — and it usually is — then you have a choice. And that matters.

U
Understand that you have a choice

This is the step most people skip — and it's the most important one. You are not your thoughts. Thoughts arise on their own, but you are the one who decides whether to follow them or let them pass. You don't have to believe everything your mind tells you, and you don't have to keep engaging with thinking that's making you feel worse. You always have the option to let go of it. Not suppress it, not fight it — just let it go, the way you'd let go of a rope you've been pulling on.

S
Say the mantra

Repeat this to yourself, quietly or out loud, as many times as you need:

"Thinking is the root cause of suffering."

This isn't about blaming yourself for having thoughts — it's a reminder that the suffering you're feeling right now is being generated by the thinking, not by reality. The situation may be hard, but the spiral is optional. Saying this aloud can help interrupt the loop and give your mind something to hold onto other than the thought it keeps returning to.

E
Experience what you're feeling — without adding more thinking to it

Once the thinking quiets a little, you may notice there are emotions underneath it — anxiety, sadness, frustration, fear. Don't push them away, and don't try to think your way through them. Just feel them. Emotions that are allowed to be there, without resistance, without analysis, tend to move through you. It's the thinking layered on top of them — the why am I feeling this, what does it mean, what should I do — that keeps them stuck. Let the emotion be there. Breathe with it. That's enough.

Repeat the process as many times as you need to. Some days it takes one round. Some days it takes five. That's not failure — that's just how it works when the nervous system is genuinely overwhelmed.

The goal isn't to never have negative thoughts again. The goal is to get faster at noticing them, choosing not to follow them, and returning to a state where you can actually function — where you can sit down, open your notes, and be present for what's in front of you.

That's what this process is for. And with practice, the pause gets shorter and the return gets easier.
