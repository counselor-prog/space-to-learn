Does the Pomodoro Technique actually work?

Students ask me this a lot. They have heard of Pomodoro, maybe tried it once, and want to know if it is actually worth building into their routine. My honest answer: it depends entirely on what kind of work you are doing and what your actual problem is.

The idea is simple. You set a timer for 25 minutes, work with no distractions, then take a five minute break. The research behind it is real in one sense: sustained attention does degrade over time, and short breaks help reset focus. So the underlying principle is not wrong.

Where I see it fall apart is with students doing complex work. If you are working through a difficult pathophysiology concept or trying to write something that requires depth and continuity, 25 minutes is often not enough time to even get into a proper flow state. You get into it, and then the timer goes off and you have to break. That interruption can actually cost you more than the break gives back.

The students I have seen get the most out of Pomodoro are the ones whose main struggle is starting. If procrastination is your problem, the technique has a real psychological trick built into it. "Just 25 minutes" is a much easier commitment to make than "I need to study for three hours." It lowers the barrier to sitting down. And often, once you sit down, you keep going past the timer anyway.

It also works well for high-volume, discrete tasks. Anki reviews, reading through lecture slides, watching a video and pausing to take notes. Things with a natural stopping point every few minutes anyway. For that kind of work, the Pomodoro structure fits nicely.

What I actually recommend instead

Match the block length to the work. For tasks that need real depth, try 50-minute blocks with a 10-minute break, or even 90-minute sessions if you can sustain them. Ninety minutes roughly matches the brain's natural ultradian rhythm, the cycle your focus tends to follow without you forcing it. For lighter review work, 25 minutes is fine.

The part of Pomodoro that I think is genuinely underrated is not the timer itself. It is the habit of tracking time deliberately. Most students I work with vastly overestimate how much focused studying they are actually doing. Sitting at a desk with your notes open and actually thinking are not the same thing. The moment you start measuring your focused time honestly, even with a simple timer, it changes how you study.

So try it. But treat the 25 minutes as a starting point, not a rule. Your brain is not a tomato, and the timer should serve you, not the other way around.
