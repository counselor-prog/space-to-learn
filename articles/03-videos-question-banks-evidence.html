Watching more videos made these students score worse

Read that title again. It is not a typo, and it is not clickbait. It is the actual finding.

A study following two full cohorts of medical students found that the more educational videos a student watched, the lower they scored on Step 1. Not slightly lower. Meaningfully, consistently lower, across both groups studied. Meanwhile, working through practice questions, actually doing the harder thing, was tied to better performance.

Sit with that for a second, because it goes against everything a study session feels like it should look like. Watching a clear, well made video feels like learning. You are focused, it makes sense, you nod along and finish feeling like you understand it. That feeling is real. What it does not reliably build is the kind of memory you can pull out under exam pressure or in front of a patient.

Here is why. Watching is passive. The information moves toward you and you receive it. You are never required to produce anything yourself. A practice question asks something completely different of you. It forces you to retrieve what you know, apply it somewhere new, and very often expose a gap you did not know you had. That retrieval effort, uncomfortable as it is, is the actual mechanism that builds usable knowledge.

I am not telling you to stop watching videos. I am telling you to stop letting them be the whole plan.

How I would use them instead

Watch a video for your very first pass on something unfamiliar. Get the shape of it, nothing more. Then move to practice questions on that exact topic within a day or two, not weeks later. If you get a question wrong, that is your cue to go clarify the specific gap, not a reason to go watch another hour of general content on the subject.

Here is a gut check I give every student. If you can watch something lying in bed with the lights off and still follow along without effort, it is probably not doing much for you. Real studying should ask something of you. If it never does, you are probably filling hours instead of building memory.
