Questions to ask yourself while you read

Reading a chapter and reading it well are not the same activity, even though they look identical from across the room. The difference is not how long you spend or how many pages you cover. It is the questions running in the back of your mind while you do it. Here is a simple set to build the habit around, broken into three natural stages.

Before you start reading

What is this actually about, in one sentence?
Why am I reading this right now? What am I supposed to walk away knowing or being able to do?
Is this describing a fact, a process, or an argument? Medical texts mix all three, and it changes how you should read it.

While you're reading

What is the main point this section is making, not just what topic is it covering?
How do I know this is true? Is it based on a study, a guideline, expert consensus, or is it just stated as fact?
Does this fit with what I already know, or does it contradict something I learned before? If it contradicts, why?
What would happen if I got this wrong with a real patient? This one is specific to medicine, and it's a good gut check for how carefully you need to lock this in versus skim it.
Am I actually understanding this, or am I just recognizing the words? If you can't explain it in your own words right now, you're probably in the second category.

After you finish a section

Could I explain this to a classmate without looking at the page?
What is still fuzzy or unclear to me here?
How does this connect to something else I've already learned? Where does it fit in the bigger picture?
If a patient asked me about this in plain language, could I answer them?
The one question that matters most: could I use this, not just recall it? Medicine isn't really testing whether you can repeat a fact back. It's testing whether you can pull the right piece of knowledge out and apply it under pressure, with a real person in front of you. Reading with that question in mind changes what you pay attention to on the page.
