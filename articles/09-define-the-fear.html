Define the fear

Most of the time, when we say we're afraid of something, we haven't actually looked at it clearly. We've felt the dread, backed away from it, and let it sit in the corner of our minds where it quietly grows larger than it needs to be.

This exercise asks you to do the opposite. To walk toward the fear, look at it directly, and examine what's actually there.

It works best with a pen and paper. Write your answers down — don't just think through them. Thinking keeps things abstract. Writing makes them real enough to work with.

Start here: What are you afraid of? Be specific.

Name it plainly. Not a vague worry, but the actual thing — the outcome, the moment, the scenario. Write it down as concretely as you can before you continue.

Then ask yourself: what you're truly afraid of is probably not the event itself, but how you think it would make you feel if it happened. What are you afraid of feeling?

Once you have that written down, work through the questions below in order. Take as much time as you need on each one.

1

If what you're afraid of happens, what are you worried it will mean about you? What conclusion have you drawn about yourself?

Try completing this sentence: "If [what you're afraid of happening] happens, then it means I am ___________."
2

Is that conclusion actually valid? Or is it a story you've been telling yourself — something you decided was true at some point, and have been carrying ever since without questioning it?

3

How does holding onto that conclusion make you feel? Sit with it. Don't rush past this one.

4

What is it costing you to keep believing this thinking? Write down as much as you can, with as much specificity as possible. Don't stay in the abstract — name the real costs. What have you avoided? What have you held back from? What has this fear taken from you? Feel it as you write.

5

How can you see that this thinking — not the situation itself, but the story you've built around it — is what's actually creating your suffering?

6

Are you ready to stop believing in this thinking and let it go?

7

How does it feel when you imagine letting go of it? Notice what happens in your body, not just in your mind.

8

What is your intuition telling you right now? Not your thinking — your intuition. What do you sense, underneath all of it?

9

How does your intuition feel compared to your thinking? Notice the difference in quality — where one tightens and one opens.

Set an intention. Whatever your intuition is pointing toward right now — commit to following it, even a little. You don't have to resolve everything today. You just have to take one step in the direction your intuition is showing you, rather than the direction your fear is pushing you.

You can return to this exercise any time the fear comes back. It usually gets shorter each time.
