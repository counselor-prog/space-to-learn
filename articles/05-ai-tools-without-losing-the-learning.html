ChatGPT is quietly making you dumber. Here's the one rule that stops it.

I am not going to tell you to stop using AI tools. That ship has sailed, and pretending otherwise would make me useless to you. But I will tell you this. Used the wrong way, these tools let you feel like you learned something without ever building the memory that actually holds up under pressure. That gap between feeling like you know it and actually knowing it is where students get hurt.

Surveys of medical students right now show usage climbing fast, and every cohort I work with is already using these tools daily, whether or not anyone gave them a framework for it. So let me give you one.

The question was never whether to use ChatGPT or tools like it. The real question is whether you are using it to skip an effortful step, or to get support after you have already done the effortful part yourself. That single distinction tells you almost everything you need to know.

Where I think these tools genuinely help

When a concept is not landing no matter how many times you read it, ask the tool to explain it a completely different way. A different analogy can unlock something a textbook never will.

Once you have already worked through the standard material on a topic, ask it to generate extra practice questions, especially for something niche that question banks barely touch.

Use it to clean up and organize notes you have already written yourself, so it is formatting your thinking, not doing your thinking.

And here is my favorite one. Write out your own explanation of a topic in your own words, then ask the tool to poke holes in it. That combines self testing with a second set of eyes, and it is one of the sharpest ways to use these tools I have seen.

Where it quietly works against you

Asking it to summarize something you have not engaged with yet skips the exact struggle that builds memory in the first place. You end up with a summary you recognize but could never reproduce under pressure.

Using it to answer practice questions instead of attempting them yourself first destroys the entire value of the question. The value was never in seeing the answer. It was in your own attempt, including getting it wrong.

And do not mistake a confident sounding answer for a correct one. These tools can sound completely certain while being wrong, especially on detailed clinical specifics. Anything you plan to rely on for an exam or a patient needs to be checked against a source you actually trust.

The one rule I want you to keep

Before you use one of these tools on something, ask yourself honestly: am I skipping the hard part, or am I getting help after doing it? If it is the first, you are probably trading real learning for a feeling of progress. If it is the second, you have likely found a genuine addition to how you study.

The students who get the most out of these tools are not the ones avoiding them out of caution, and they are not the ones using them for everything either. They are the ones who decided where the line was before the deadline pressure decided it for them.
