Is AI quietly switching off your thinking?

I want to walk you through something bigger than study technique for a moment, because it is going to shape your entire career, not just your next exam.

Researchers now have a name for what happens when we hand a thinking task to a machine instead of doing it ourselves. It is called cognitive offloading, and a growing body of research is finding a consistent pattern: people who lean on AI tools heavily and often tend to score lower on measures of critical thinking than people who use them lightly or occasionally. One large study found this negative relationship held up even after accounting for age and education, and it found younger, more AI dependent users were the most affected group.

A 2026 report from a university network focused on digital education put it plainly. AI is especially risky for people still building their foundational skills, because older technologies like calculators and GPS mainly offloaded storage and retrieval, things like remembering a fact or a route. Generative AI offloads something deeper: the actual reasoning. Synthesizing, evaluating, and judging are the exact skills clinical reasoning is built on, and they are exactly the skills at risk if you let a tool do that thinking for you before you have built it yourself.

Here is the part I think matters most for you specifically. Some research has also found the opposite is possible. When AI is used to prompt deeper engagement, for example by asking it to critique your own reasoning or debate a position with you, it can actually strengthen critical thinking rather than erode it. The tool itself is not the problem. How you use it is.

What this looks like in medicine specifically

Every field has its own version of this risk, but medicine has particularly high stakes. A wrong answer confidently generated by a tool that sounds certain, but is not checked against something reliable, is not just an academic slip. It is the exact failure mode we cannot afford in a clinical setting. Building strong independent reasoning now, before AI becomes second nature in practice, is not optional. It is protective.

A simple test I want you to run on yourself

Before you lean on a tool for something, ask whether you are using it to reason through a problem, or to avoid reasoning through it. Explaining your own thinking out loud, then asking a tool to challenge or stress test it, keeps you in the driver's seat. Asking it to just tell you the answer hands the wheel over entirely, and that is where the erosion happens, quietly, one shortcut at a time.

None of this is about fear. It is about staying the one doing the thinking, in a field where your thinking is the entire job.
