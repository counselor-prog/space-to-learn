The study plan that only works on your best week isn't a plan

Here is a hard truth I tell every student before clerkship starts. If your study system only survives your best weeks, you don't actually have a system. You have a hope, and clerkship is going to find every hole in it within the first month.

Every student I have ever worked with can build a study routine when life is calm. The real test is whether that same routine survives the first week it is not.

I learned this the hard way supervising tutors and watching students juggle caseloads, jobs, and life happening around them all at once. The plan that only works on your best week is not a plan. It is a hope. And clerkship will find every gap in a hope within the first month.

Spaced review sounds simple. Study something, come back to it a few days later, then a couple weeks later, then a month later. Each pass makes it stick a little more and takes a little less time. In theory, beautiful. In practice, the first twelve hour shift followed by an early morning wipes out the whole plan, because the plan depended on a version of your week that no longer exists.

Here is what actually holds up

Stop anchoring your review to a time of day. Anchor it to something that already happens no matter what kind of day you had. Right after you eat. Right before you sleep. Right when you sit down on the bus. Time based plans die the moment your hours shift. Habit based plans survive it.

Keep it short on purpose. I mean genuinely short. Ten minutes. Not because ten minutes is all you need, but because ten minutes is something you can protect even on your worst day. An hour long plan only survives your good days. A ten minute plan survives all of them, and you can always do more when you have the energy for it.

Let a tool carry the scheduling weight for you. Something like Anki decides what comes back to you and when, based on how well you knew it last time. On the days you have zero capacity to plan anything, that decision being made for you already is the difference between reviewing and not.

And do not let old material quietly fall off the list. It is tempting during a hard rotation to only look at whatever is directly in front of you. But the material you already worked hard to learn earlier is exactly what fades first if you stop touching it. Protecting it is not optional, it is the whole point of spacing in the first place.

The honest version, not the perfect one

I am not asking you to protect a perfect system through clerkship. Nobody does that. I am asking you to protect the smallest version of the system, the one so small it survives your worst week, not just your best one. That is the version that actually gets you to the exam with everything still intact.
