Why medical students stop believing in themselves

I have worked with a lot of medical students who, on paper, have no reason to doubt themselves. They got into a competitive program. They are smart, hardworking, and genuinely care about what they are doing. And yet, sitting across from me, they cannot hold onto the belief that they are capable. Every exam feels like the one that will finally expose them. Every clinical rotation feels like borrowed time.

This is not a confidence problem in the vague, motivational sense. It has a name. It is called low self-efficacy, and understanding what it actually is changes how you address it.

What self-efficacy actually means

Albert Bandura defined it as your belief in your capacity to execute the specific behaviors needed to produce a specific outcome. The important word is specific. Self-efficacy is not a general feeling about yourself. A student can feel completely capable in anatomy and fall apart the moment they have to talk to a patient. That is not inconsistency or weakness. That is how self-efficacy works. It is task-specific, which means addressing it has to be task-specific too.

The problem is that medical training often does not treat it that way. When a student struggles in one area, the doubt can bleed into everything. And the training environment, which I will come back to, is often very good at accelerating that process.

What actually moves it

The research here is clear, and it matches what I have seen in practice. The most powerful thing is what Bandura called mastery experiences. Not being told you can do something. Not visualizing success. Actually doing a hard thing and coming out the other side. The experience of surviving it.

This is why simulation labs and OSCEs, when they are structured well, work. They are manufacturing small wins under controlled conditions before the real stakes arrive. The student practices the skill, gets through it, and the brain registers: I did that. That registration is what builds the belief. Reassurance from someone else cannot do the same thing. It is thin. It fades. The experience sticks in a way that words do not.

The second source is vicarious experience. Watching someone like you succeed. Not an attending who has been practicing for twenty years. Someone one year ahead, same doubts, similar background, managing it. I think peer mentorship is one of the most underused tools in medical education precisely because of this. Students often feel like they are the only one struggling, and seeing someone who looked like them from the outside -- and felt like them on the inside -- making it through shifts something real.

The third one is physiological, and medical culture almost completely ignores it. Chronic sleep deprivation, which medical training is essentially built on, directly tanks self-efficacy. What happens is that the student feels exhausted, anxious, foggy, and interprets that state as evidence of incompetence. The body is sending distress signals and the mind turns them into a verdict: I am not cut out for this. Addressing the body is addressing the belief. They are not separate problems.

What does not work

Reassurance, on its own, does very little. I want to be direct about this because a lot of wellbeing support in medical schools stops here. Telling someone they are capable without giving them the experience of being capable is not enough. The student nods, leaves the office, and the doubt is back the next morning because nothing has actually changed in their lived experience.

The harder thing to say

Some of what erodes self-efficacy in medical students is not about the individual at all. The training environment itself, the hierarchy, the public correction, the culture of pimping, the moving goalposts, is often structured in ways that are genuinely efficacy-destroying. Individual strategies help. But they are working against a real headwind.

I am not saying that to make it hopeless. I am saying it because I think students deserve to know the full picture. When you are doubting yourself in that environment, part of what you are responding to is the environment. That is not weakness. That is a very human response to a very difficult set of conditions.

The students I have seen rebuild their belief in themselves did it through accumulation. Small supervised wins. Honest conversations with peers who were further along. Protecting sleep more than the culture told them to. And getting specific about where the doubt actually lived, rather than letting it spread into everything.

You do not need to feel ready before you start. You need to start enough times that the evidence catches up with the doubt.
